Rethinking the Cosmos

What if the mystery of missing mass in galaxies isn't about some new, exotic particle? What if it's about the fundamental nature of gravity itself?

The Galactic Speed Trap

One of the biggest puzzles in astrophysics is that galaxies spin much faster than they should. Based on the stars and gas we can see, the outer stars should be flung away into space. This interactive chart shows the problem.

Three Competing Ideas

To solve the mystery of the spinning galaxies, scientists have proposed different explanations. Here's a look at the conventional theory and two major alternatives that modify gravity.

Theory 1: Dark Matter and Dark Energy

âš›

The most widely accepted cosmological model states that the universe is dominated by two invisible components: **Dark Matter** and **Dark Energy**. Dark Matter provides the extra gravitational pull needed to hold fast-spinning galaxies together. **Dark Energy**, on the other hand, is the mysterious force accelerating the expansion of the entire universe. Despite their combined dominance, neither has been directly detected.

Theory 2: Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND)

âš–

The **MOND** hypothesis suggests that Newton's law of gravity changes at very low accelerations, typical of the outer regions of galaxies. Instead of invoking 'invisible mass,' MOND modifies the force law itself, causing stars to orbit faster than expected. While successful at explaining galaxy rotation curves, MOND has struggled to universally explain the dynamics of galaxy clusters and cosmological observations.

This "self-interaction" could amplify its own force, providing the extra pull to hold galaxies together without needing any invisible matter.

Spacetime as a Trampoline

Imagine spacetime is a stretched trampoline. Einstein's theory says a bowling ball (like a star) placed on it creates a dip, causing marbles (planets) to circle around it. This is standard gravity.

Now, imagine the fabric of the trampoline itself has some "stickiness." The dip created by the bowling ball pulls the surrounding fabric inward, making the dip even deeper and wider than expected. This extra pull from the fabric is analogous to **General Relativity Self-Interaction (GRSI)**. The energy of the gravitational field itself contributes to the warping of spacetime.

In our theory, this effect is negligible in places like our solar system but becomes significant at the immense scale of a galaxy, potentially explaining the extra gravitational force we observe.

Searching for Signatures

We can't "see" **GRSI** directly, but we can look for its effects. Our work involves running complex computer simulations and comparing them to astronomical observations. We look for:

  • Specific Galaxy Rotation Curves: **GRSI** predicts a unique shape for the velocity curve of galaxies that differs subtly from the predictions of most dark matter models.
  • Gravitational Lensing: How light from distant galaxies is bent by the gravity of closer galaxies. **GRSI** might produce unique lensing patterns because it alters the distribution of gravity.
  • Galaxy Cluster Dynamics: Studying how galaxies move within large clusters can also reveal anomalies that might favor one theory over another.

What GRSI Predicts

A good scientific theory makes testable predictions. Here are some key predictions from **GRSI** that differentiate it from the standard Dark Matter model:

  • No Dark Matter Particles: The most obvious prediction is that experiments designed to find dark matter particles (like those in deep underground labs) will continue to find nothing.
  • Universal Behavior: **GRSI** suggests that the relationship between a galaxy's visible mass and its rotation speed should follow a very specific, universal law, with less variation than some dark matter models might allow.
  • Modifications to Cosmology: Our theories may also change predictions for the evolution of the universe on the largest scales, such as the Cosmic Microwave Background radiation, which we are actively investigating.
